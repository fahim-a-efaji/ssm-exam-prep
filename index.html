L1 Introducing Scrum in SAFe Domain 1 · 22–28% ▶
4 Agile Manifesto Values
  • Individuals & interactions over processes and tools
  • Working software over comprehensive documentation
  • Customer collaboration over contract negotiation
  • Responding to change over following a plan
5 Scrum Values + 3 Pillars
  • Values: Courage · Commitment · Focus · Respect · Openness
  • Pillars: Transparency · Inspection · Adaptation

The Scrum TeamAgile Team
Agile Team Basics
  • Cross-functional, self-organizing, ≤10 members
  • Two specialty roles: Scrum Master and Product Owner
  • Iterations: same length, back-to-back, typically 2 weeks
  • Goal: deliver working, tested functionality at end of each iteration
5 Built-in Quality XP Practices
  • Continuous Integration · Test-First (TDD/BDD) · Refactoring · Pair Work · Collective Ownership
4 SAFe Core Values
  • Alignment · Built-in Quality · Transparency · Program Execution
Work Item Hierarchy
  • Stories — fit in 1 iteration (days)
  • Features — fit in 1 PI within 1 ART (weeks); have benefit hypothesis + acceptance criteria
  • Epics — span multiple PIs and ARTs (months)
⚡ Key differentiators from Waterfall: Incremental delivery of value + Fast Feedback (not Architectural Runway)
⚠️ Lean-Agile Mindset = Lean Thinking + Embracing Agility — NOT Systems Thinking
L2 The Scrum Master / Team Coach Role Domain 2 · 26–30% ▶
SM is Above All Else: A Servant Leader
  • Uses persuasion, not authority to influence
  • Listens to and supports team members in problem identification and decision-making
  • Thinks beyond day-to-day activities
  • Seeks to help without diminishing the commitment of others
  • Encourages personal development of each individual
5 SM Responsibilities
  • 1. Facilitate PI Planning
  • 2. Support iterations (all events)
  • 3. Improve flow
  • 4. Build a high-performing team
  • 5. Improve ART performance

Tuckman's Team Development Stages
  • Forming → team comes together, roles unclear
  • Storming → conflict emerges (normal and healthy!)
  • Norming → members stop focusing on own goals, develop better ways of working together
  • Performing → team is highly productive and self-managing
Lencioni's 5 Dysfunctions (base → top)
  • 1. Absence of Trust → SAFe addresses: Scrum creates safe environment
  • 2. Fear of Conflict → SAFe addresses: Healthy conflict enabled in Scrum
  • 3. Lack of Commitment → SAFe addresses: Shared iteration & PI commitments
  • 4. Avoidance of Accountability → SAFe addresses: Peer pressure & results review
  • 5. Inattention to Results → SAFe addresses: Iteration Reviews & Retrospectives
High-Performing Team Attributes
  • Self-organizing · Aligned and collaborative · Open and clear communication
  • Safe atmosphere to take risks · Value diversity · Mutual trust
  • Effective decision-making · Effective timely feedback · Understand work's impact
Conflict Resolution Steps
  • 1. Meet with the conflicting parties
  • 2. Identify exactly what each party wants
  • 3. Identify why each party needs it
  • 4. Obtain agreement that the common goal is correct
  • 5. Review and challenge assumptions
  • 6. Find what common goal ties these reasons together

L3 PI Planning (Experiencing PI Planning) Domain 4 · Heavily tested ▶
PI Planning at a Glance
  • 2-day event · Facilitated by RTE · All ART teams attend in person
  • Held every PI (~8–12 weeks; 10 weeks typical)
  • Product Management owns feature priorities; Development teams own story planning
  • "The heartbeat of the ART" — most critical alignment event

ROAM Risk Categories
  • R — Resolved: addressed; no longer a concern
  • O — Owned: someone has taken responsibility for the risk
  • A — Accepted: nothing more can be done; may compromise the release
  • M — Mitigated: team has a plan to reduce or eliminate the threat
PI Objectives: Committed vs Stretch
  • Committed: firm delivery commitments; counted in ART predictability
  • Stretch (Uncommitted): desired but no firm commitment; planned BV = 0
  • Stretch objectives let teams signal capacity without distorting predictability metrics
Three Cs of a User Story
  • Card — written representation in tool or on card
  • Conversation — details come from discussion with PO
  • Confirmation — acceptance criteria confirms story's correctness
⚠️ Anti-pattern: detailed plan becomes the goal rather than alignment; pressuring teams to overcommit
⚡ Fist of Five: 1–2 = stop and resolve concerns; 3 = concerns but will support; 4–5 = committed

Iteration Planning Agenda (5 Steps)
  • 1. Establish capacity & confirm capacity allocation guidelines
  • 2. Story analysis and normalized estimation
  • 3. Identify tasks for each story (optional — tasking)
  • 4. Draft iteration goals
  • 5. Commit to iteration goals
3 Team Sync Questions
  • What did I do yesterday to advance iteration goals?
  • What will I do today to advance iteration goals?
  • Are there impediments preventing the team from meeting goals?
Velocity Rules
  • Only fully completed stories count — zero partial credit
  • Example: A(8) done + B(1) done + C(5) at 50% → Velocity = 9
  • Use historical velocity to forecast future iterations
8 Flow Accelerators
  • 1. Visualize & limit WIP · 2. Address bottlenecks · 3. Minimize handoffs & dependencies
  • 4. Get faster feedback · 5. Work in smaller batches · 6. Reduce queue lengths
  • 7. Optimize time "in the zone" · 8. Remediate legacy policies & practices
CALMR — SAFe's DevOps Approach
  • C — Culture (shared responsibility for dev, deployment, ops)
  • A — Automation (automate the Continuous Delivery Pipeline)
  • L — Lean flow (keep batch sizes small, limit WIP, extreme visibility)
  • M — Measurement (full-stack telemetry through the pipeline)
  • R — Recovery (fast fix-forward, fast reversion, low-risk releases)
3 Flow Metrics
  • Flow Velocity: backlog items (story points) completed per iteration
  • Flow Load: items currently in the system (visualized via Cumulative Flow Diagram)
  • Flow Distribution: types of work (stories/enablers/maintenance) over time
⚠️ Retro anti-pattern: inviting management or outside stakeholders
⚡ Improvement items from Retro → enter the Team Backlog as actionable stories
L5 Finishing the PI (IP Iteration & I&A) Domain 4 · ART Events ▶
IP Iteration — 3 Purposes + Anti-Pattern
  • Innovation: hackathons, spikes, supplier showcases, skill development, infrastructure improvements
  • Planning: PI Planning preparation; hosts PI Planning and I&A events
  • Buffer: estimating guard band ensuring cadence-based delivery predictability
  • ⛔ Anti-pattern: planning regular feature work for IP during PI Planning
Consequences of NOT Having IP Iteration
  • No delivery buffer → unpredictability · Little innovation ("tyranny of the urgent")
  • Technical debt grows uncontrollably · People burn out
  • No time for teams to plan, demo, or improve together
Inspect & Adapt — 3 Parts (3–4 hrs; Teams + Stakeholders)
  • 1. PI System Demo: demonstrate current solution state; Business Owners must attend
  • 2. Quantitative & Qualitative Measurement: ART predictability measure; target 80–100%
  • 3. Problem-Solving Workshop: root-cause analysis → improvement Features
Problem-Solving Workshop — 6 Steps
  • 1. Agree on the problem to solve (ALWAYS first)
  • 2. Apply root cause analysis (5 Whys / fishbone diagram)
  • 3. Identify the biggest root cause
  • 4. Restate the new problem for the biggest root cause
  • 5. Brainstorm solutions
  • 6. Identify improvement backlog items
ART Predictability Measure
  • Compares actual business value achieved vs. planned
  • Target: 80–100% = effective process control
  • Stretch objectives have planned BV = 0 (doesn't distort the measure)
  • Teams self-assess with Business Owners at the PI System Demo
⚡ Spikes = exploration enabler stories to reduce technical risk or clarify a requirement
❌ I&A anti-pattern: no actionable improvement Features created from the workshop
L6 AI for Scrum Masters + Critical Exam Terms Lesson 6 + Glossary ▶
Responsible AI for Scrum Masters
  • AI augments — does NOT replace — the SM's coaching role
  • Key risks: Hallucinations · Bias · Data privacy
  • Always verify AI output with human judgment
  • SM remains accountable; AI is a tool, not a decision-maker
